Lightning Network Considerations:

Lightning Network wallets allow for fast, low-fee Bitcoin transactions, but have some unique considerations:

  • Channel management - Some wallets handle this automatically, others require manual management
  • Liquidity - Your ability to send/receive depends on your channel capacity
  • Online requirement - Most Lightning wallets need to be online to receive payments
  • Backup complexity - Lightning requires backing up channel states, not just seed words

For beginners, consider custodial or hybrid solutions like Phoenix or Breez that simplify the experience.

Via bitcoin-only.com, choose an On-chain and/or Lightning wallet for your platform (Desktop, Android or iOS). Install it on your phone/computer

Be aware that you will get a backup phrase. If your phone is broken/stolen you cannot recover the coins without this backup phrase. So store it well, on paper (never digitally), possibly in a safe?

For tax purposes, consider using wallet software that can export transaction history. Keep records of all transactions, including dates, amounts, and purposes.

  • Hardware wallets - Purpose-built devices for secure key storage

Inheritance Planning for Bitcoin:

Consider creating an "inheritance packet" that includes:

  • Instructions for accessing your bitcoin
  • Location of hardware wallets and seed backups
  • Basic explanation of how Bitcoin works
  • Contact information for a trusted Bitcoin-knowledgeable person

Options for inheritance planning:

  • Dead man's switch - Automated system that requires periodic check-ins
  • Multisig with trusted parties - Requires multiple people to access funds
  • Time-locked transactions - Set up transactions that only become valid after a certain time
  • Legal arrangements - Work with an attorney to include Bitcoin in your will/trust

A multisig consists of multiple (hardware) wallets and X-of-N transactions. You could have 5 wallets for example, and need 3 of them to sign your transaction (= send your bitcoin). You can pick any numbers you'd like. Higher numbers means more action is needed. Multisig is often considered the holy grail of self-custody security. It's also used within big custodial companies and exchanges to make sure not 1 single person can access the bitcoin, for example 3 wallets are needed (maybe the CEO, CFO and a dude named Ben always need to sign)

For all actions you do with your multisig-secured bitcoin you need collaboration of multiple parties. This means more work is needed.

Multisigs are very cool

  1. Decide how many parties (people) you want to involve in total. This is your variable N
  2. Decide how many of these parties you want to have at minimum to sign transactions (send bitcoin) this is variable X
  3. Find a hardware wallet that supports multisig schemes, buy N of these wallets
  4. Set up your X-of-N multisig using the wallets
  5. Distribute the wallets over multiple parties
  6. Make sure you document the needed steps well, so in 10 years you still know how to use the multisig

Losing too many parts or forgetting how multisig works are the bigger risks. Make sure people understand what their part is/means. Document this well. Consider scheduling a yearly check to make sure everyone is still in possession of their wallets and understands.

Privacy Best Practices:

  • Avoid address reuse - Use a new address for each transaction
  • Use coin control - Manually select which UTXOs to spend
  • Consider CoinJoin - Mix your coins to break transaction history
  • Use Tor/VPN - Hide your IP address when using Bitcoin
  • Avoid KYC when possible - Consider P2P exchanges for privacy
  • Separate identities - Don't link all your Bitcoin activity

Remember that Bitcoin's blockchain is public and permanent. Once privacy is lost, it's very difficult to regain.
